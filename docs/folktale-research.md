## Tales used in the game

### Chapter 1 — The Sun and the Moon (Hawkes 1916, p.156)
A young woman is visited in the dark of the communal house by a man who hides his
identity. She marks him with soot to know him by daylight, discovers who he is,
and flees through the sky with a torch; he chases her. She becomes the Sun, he
the Moon (dimmer, his torch having gone out), and they chase each other still.
- **Community:** Nunatsiavut broadly (Labrador telling). Pan-Inuit in origin.
- **Sensitivity:** The traditional versions carry an incest / dark-visitor
  assault theme. The in-game retelling is deliberately softened for learners.
  Flag for elder review.

### Chapter 2 — The Orphan Boy and the Moon-Man (Hawkes 1916, p.158)
A mistreated orphan sleeps in the cold entrance passage with the dogs and eats
only scraps; only the youngest girl is kind to him. He begs the Moon-Man, who
descends, whips him on the beach, and with every stroke the boy grows stronger,
until he can throw great boulders and stand up to those who abused him.
- **Community:** Nunatsiavut broadly (northern Labrador coast). Strongly
  Labrador-documented.
- **Sensitivity:** Contains child mistreatment and corporal punishment
  (flogging). Framed gently in-game. Flag for review.

### Chapter 3 — Torngâsoak & Superguksoak, Masters of Sea and Land (Hawkes 1916, p.124)
The Labrador Inuit revered **Torngâsoak**, master of the sea animals (seals,
whales), and **Superguksoak**, mistress of the land animals (caribou). When
hunting failed, the animals were understood to have been withheld — often for a
broken taboo — and only an angakok (shaman) could journey in spirit to appease
the masters and release the game. At Killiniq an "Old-woman-who-lived-in-the-sea"
was also known.
- **NOT the "Sedna" narrative** — the maiden-thrown-from-the-boat story is
  Central-Arctic/Baffin & Greenlandic and must not be presented as Labrador.
- **Community:** Killiniq / northern Labrador; Nunatsiavut broadly.
- **Sensitivity:** Highest of the set — living/traditional spiritual & shamanic
  material. Strongly recommend Nunatsiavut Government / Torngâsok review.

### Chapter 4 — The Northern Lights in the Labradorite (Jason Sikoak, Rigolet; Inuktitut Magazine #125, 2019)
A shaman coveted the northern lights and trapped them in the coastal rock,
leaving the people in darkness. A hunter shattered the stone with his harpoon to
free the lights back to the sky, but some colour stayed locked in the stone —
which is why labradorite still flashes with the colours of the aurora.
- **Community:** Rigolet, Nunatsiavut; northern Labrador coast.
- **Sensitivity:** This is a **named, living Inuk author's** retelling.
  Attribution to Jason Sikoak is essential; the in-game text is a brief
  high-level framing (not a reproduction) and permission should be sought for
  any publication/commercial reuse.

## Overall cultural-review caveat

These tales are the living cultural heritage of the Labrador Inuit, not
free-to-use content. Public-domain ethnography (Hawkes 1916) was collected by an
outsider and may carry colonial framing or material a community now treats as
restricted; contemporary retellings belong to identifiable living authors.
**Before any publication, the tales, retellings, and framing should be reviewed
and ideally co-developed with the Nunatsiavut Government and the Torngâsok
Cultural Centre / Illusuak Cultural Centre**, following "nothing about us without
us." Flag especially the shamanic material (Chapter 3) and the assault/incest and
corporal-punishment content (Chapters 1–2) for elder review.
